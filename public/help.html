Astrometric Scale Engine Interactive Relativistic Spaceflight Calculator — User Guide

Configure an origin, destination, and propulsion system to instantly compute relativistic travel times, energy requirements, and mission trajectories across any scale — from our solar system to the edge of the galaxy.

Overview

The Astrometric Scale Engine is a physics-accurate mission planning tool that solves one fundamental question: how long does it actually take to get there, and what does it cost?

It applies the real equations of special relativity for sublight travel and the Alcubierre flat-metric model for hypothetical warp drive. All durations, velocities, and energies update in real time as you adjust the controls.

⚡ Sublight Physics

True relativistic kinematics — proper time, coordinate time, time dilation, and kinetic energy are calculated from first principles using constant proper acceleration and an optional coasting phase.

🌀 Warp Drive

The Alcubierre metric model: the ship's interior remains in flat space-time (Lorentz factor = 1) while the metric itself contracts ahead and expands behind. Superluminal travel is possible but speculative.

🌌 Real Stellar Data

The Interstellar catalog sources data from the HYG star database (1,400+ named stars), the NASA Exoplanet Archive (1,500+ confirmed exoplanets), and the Messier catalog of deep-sky objects.

🪐 Solar System

The Systemic catalog uses real orbital ephemeris data from JPL Horizons — planets, dwarf planets, asteroids, and active spacecraft at their actual 3D positions in AU.

Quick Start

  1. Choose an Astrometric Regime in the left column — Systemic for travel within our solar system, Interstellar for deep-space journeys.
  2. Select an Origin Node (your departure point) from the dropdown. Use the search box to filter by name or constellation.
  3. Select a Destination Node. The 3D viewport will immediately draw a travel vector between the two points.
  4. Choose an Engine Core Tier from Tier 1 (Chemical Rocket) to Tier 5 (Alcubierre Warp).
  5. Adjust Acceleration, Velocity Ceiling, and Hull Mass using the sliders. Results update instantly in the header and right-side Telemetry HUD.

Astrometric Regime

The regime selector determines which catalog is active and which unit system is used for distance calculations.

🪐 Systemic (Local / AU)

Displays solar system bodies — planets, dwarf planets, asteroids, and spacecraft. Coordinates are in Astronomical Units (AU). Recommended for sublight, near-future propulsion tiers.

🌌 Interstellar (Deep Space / PC)

Displays the full stellar catalog. Coordinates are in Parsecs (pc). Use for any journey beyond the heliopause. Best combined with Tier 4 (Antimatter) or Tier 5 (Warp).

⚠️

Switching regimes clears your Origin and Destination selections, since the two catalogs use incompatible coordinate systems. You will need to re-select both nodes after switching.

Target Coordinates

Origin Node

Your departure point. Select from the dropdown after optionally filtering by name, constellation, or catalog category. Once selected, a card appears below the dropdown showing the object's distance from Sol and a contextual description.

Destination Node

Your arrival point. Works identically to Origin. The engine computes the 3D Euclidean distance between the two coordinate vectors in the active unit system and converts to meters for all physics calculations.

Kinematic Configuration

Acceleration Profile (Sublight only)

Sets the constant proper acceleration in multiples of Earth's surface gravity (G = 9.80665 m/s²). A value of 1.0 G is comfortable for human passengers and is the baseline used in most hard sci-fi references. The slider range is clamped to the selected engine tier's maximum.

Velocity Ceiling (Sublight only)

The maximum speed the ship is permitted to reach, expressed as a fraction of the speed of light (c). For low-tier engines where the absolute speed is small, the display switches automatically to km/h for readability.

ℹ️

Coasting Phase: If the ship reaches its velocity ceiling before the halfway point of the journey, it enters a constant-velocity coast phase. The engine then decelerates symmetrically for the second half. You can see this "trapezoidal" profile in the Velocity vs. Time chart.

Warp Factor (Warp only)

Sets the superluminal speed as a direct multiple of c. A Warp Factor of 10 means the ship traverses 10 light-years per year of coordinate time. Range is 1× to 50×.

Hull Structural Mass

The dry mass of the ship in metric tonnes (fuel and payload excluded). This affects the energy calculation. The slider is logarithmic — small adjustments near the low end have a large relative effect. A ship classification label is shown based on the mass:

Flight Charts

Two SVG charts are rendered in the lower centre column and update in real time:

Velocity vs. Time

Plots the ship's speed (as a fraction of c) over coordinate time. For sublight flights, this shows the triangular (flip-and-decelerate) or trapezoidal (with coasting phase) profile. For warp flights, the chart shows an instantaneous jump to the warp velocity and a flat line for the duration of travel.

Position vs. Time

Plots distance covered (in the active unit system) over coordinate time. For sublight, the curve is concave (slow at start, fast at peak) and symmetric. For warp, it's a straight diagonal line reflecting constant apparent velocity.

Relativistic Travel Explained

At high velocities, time passes more slowly for a moving observer than for a stationary one. This effect — time dilation — is given by the Lorentz factor:

γ = 1 / √(1 − v²/c²)

The engine uses the constant-proper-acceleration equations of special relativity. "Proper acceleration" means the acceleration felt by the crew (what a scale would measure onboard). For an acceleration of a over coordinate distance x:

  • Coordinate time (Earth observer): t = (c/a) × √( (1 + ax/c²)² − 1 )
  • Proper time (ship crew): τ = (c/a) × acosh(1 + ax/c²)
ℹ️

Example: At a continuous 1G acceleration, a trip to Proxima Centauri (4.24 ly) takes about 3.5 years of ship time but 5.9 years of Earth time. The crew ages less than the people they left behind.

At very high Lorentz factors (e.g., Antimatter tier, 0.99c), γ ≈ 7. A 50-year Earth journey takes only ~7 ship years. The crew would return to an Earth 50 years older, having aged only 7 years.

Warp Drive Physics

The Alcubierre warp metric (1994) is a theoretical solution to the Einstein field equations. The ship does not move through space — instead, the metric (the fabric of spacetime) contracts ahead of the ship and expands behind it. Within the warp bubble, the interior remains flat Minkowski spacetime:

  • No onboard acceleration — the crew experience weightlessness.
  • Lorentz factor = 1 — there is no time dilation. Ship time equals coordinate time.
  • Apparent velocity is a direct multiple of c (Warp Factor × c).
⚠️

The energy model for warp in this simulator is speculative. The real Alcubierre metric would require exotic matter with negative energy density. The Exajoule output shown is an arbitrary reactor-upkeep model and is not physically derived.

Data Catalogs

Systemic Catalog

Generated from the JPL Horizons ephemeris service. Each body has its actual 3D Cartesian position (in AU, centred on Sol) sampled across a range spanning its orbital period. The orbit path is stored as a sequence of positions for 3D rendering. Included bodies: Sol, 8 planets, Moon, 5 dwarf planets, and 8 asteroids.

Interstellar Catalog

A merged dataset from three sources:

  1. HYG Database v3.6 — over 119,000 stars from the Henry Draper, Yale, and Hipparcos catalogs, filtered to those with proper names or Bayer designations bright enough to be of interest (mag < 5.0 or proper-named). 3D positions in parsecs relative to Sol.
  2. NASA Exoplanet Archive — confirmed exoplanets within 100 parsecs, retrieved via the TAP SQL service. Includes mass estimates where known.
  3. Curated anchors — Messier objects and notable spacecraft waypoints with hand-written narratives.

Glossary

TermDefinition
Proper Time (τ)Time as experienced by the traveller aboard the ship. Always ≤ coordinate time at relativistic speeds.
Coordinate Time (t)Time measured by a stationary observer (e.g., on Earth). The "clock on the wall" perspective.
Lorentz Factor (γ)The ratio of coordinate time to proper time. At 0.99c, γ ≈ 7.09. At 0.9999c, γ ≈ 70.7.
Proper AccelerationThe acceleration felt inside the ship (what a scale measures). Distinct from coordinate acceleration at relativistic speeds.
Flip-and-DecelerateThe standard deep-space manoeuvre: accelerate for the first half of the journey, flip 180°, decelerate for the second half, arriving with near-zero velocity.
Coasting PhaseWhen the ship reaches its velocity ceiling before the halfway point, it coasts at constant speed until it must begin decelerating.
Alcubierre MetricA solution to the Einstein field equations describing a "warp bubble" that permits apparent faster-than-light travel without local violations of relativity.
AUAstronomical Unit. The average Earth–Sun distance. 1 AU ≈ 149.6 million km.
Parsec (pc)1 parsec = 3.26 light-years = 3.086 × 10¹⁶ metres. Standard unit of stellar distance.
Light-year (ly)The distance light travels in one year ≈ 9.461 × 10¹⁵ metres.
Exajoule (EJ)10¹⁸ joules. For reference: the entire world's annual energy consumption is approximately 580 EJ.
